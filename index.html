About Me

The first memory of my childhood I can remember is sitting in front of a TV, watching movies on VHS tapes, disks, and strange channels. I sometimes joke that movies taught me how to live (of course, my mother hates that joke), but it’s true. Watching films isn’t just entertainment for me - it’s a way of learning, of living other lives, of going through different experiences.

At the core of every film is a script written by someone who went through something and decided to shape it into a story. That’s why everything I see on screen - the characters, the stories, the dialogues - makes sense to me. They’re built on something real.

Unpacking a film that stays with me is more than writing - it’s experiencing, feeling, analyzing. And I want to share that with you.

  • Blood, Memory, and the Sacred Hunger for Meaning: On Jim Jarmusch's Only Lovers Left Alive

    This piece is on Jim Jarmusch’s Only Lovers Left Alive, where blood becomes more than survival, it is ritual, devotion, and remembrance. Between Detroit’s ruins and Tangier’s breath, Adam and Eve, two weary immortals, search for what still feels sacred in a world that has forgotten how to pray. In this essay, I reflect on art, faith, and the quiet endurance of love - how to keep beauty alive when meaning itself begins to fade.

  • The Siren of Naples: On Beauty, Time, and Sorrentino’s Parthenope

    This piece is on Paolo Sorrentino’s Parthenope as both myth and mirror. In this essay, I explore beauty as both gift and wound, the inevitability of time, and Naples as a city where myth bleeds into memory. Parthenope becomes a modern siren whose song is not desire but impermanence, reminding us that every summer must fade, and nothing gold can stay.

  • The Imagination that Devours Itself: Loneliness in I’m Thinking of Ending Things

    In this essay, I reflect on Charlie Kaufman's I'm Thinking of Ending Things - a quiet tragedy of a man who builds an entire world in his mind to survive loneliness, where imagination becomes both sanctuary and burden, and memory blurs into longing.

  • Searching for the Unseen: La Chimera and the Ache of What Can’t Be Held

    In this piece I explore grief, faith, and the beauty of longing without resolution. The story is a quiet meditation on Arthur, a tomb raider not in search of gold but of a memory - a ghostly presence bound to the earth by a red thread. Through ruins, puddles, and silence, he grieves not to reclaim the past, but simply to be near it. La Chimera becomes a reflection on ghayb, the Islamic concept of the unseen - what cannot be touched or explained, but is deeply felt.

  • Taste of Cherry: A Story on Wanting to Leave and Learning to Stay

    This piece reflects on Taste of Cherry not as a film about death, but as a meditation on the quiet, uncertain space before it. Through the story of Mr. Badii and his search for someone to bury him, I explore how cinema can hold our silences, our grief, and even our mercy. Inspired by Islamic thought and personal reflection, it is an essay about staying, listening, and how something as simple as a fruit – a mulberry – can be a sign to stay.

  • How Films Shape Our Understanding of Reality: A Close-Up on Abbas Kiarostami

    This article unpacks Abbas Kiarostami’s Close-Up as a poetic and philosophical inquiry into identity, deception, and our hunger to be seen. It explores how cinema can become a mirror, not just a lens – asking us to question what’s real, what’s performance, and how compassion emerges when we choose to listen instead of judge. Drawing on narrative theory and Islamic ethics, it argues that Sabzian’s “fraud” was in fact a cry to belong – and that art can offer a kind of truth no confession can.
